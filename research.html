Research

My research aims to understand how emotion, reward, and expectation shape cognitive control. I combine behavioural experiments, eye-tracking, neuroimaging, and computational modelling to examine how proactive control mechanisms resolve affective interference in the brain and behaviour.

Reward & Emotional Conflict

Examining how reward motivation can modulate emotional conflict in the brain and behaviour.

  • Paradigm: An emotional conflict task is preceded by reward vs. no‑reward cues. Participants (n ≈ 40) identify facial expressions (happy/fear) while ignoring incongruent emotion words.
  • Measurements: Behaviour (accuracy, reaction time) and 3T fMRI were recorded to capture both behavioural performance and neural activation patterns during conflict resolution.
  • Analysis: Multivariate pattern analysis (linear SVM) and representational similarity analysis (RSA) were applied to fMRI data; behavioural conflict costs were compared across reward vs. no‑reward blocks.
  • Findings: Reward cues attenuated conflict costs and increased preparatory activation in fronto‑striatal and limbic regions (ACC, ventral striatum), highlighting motivation‑driven proactive control.

Expectancy & Proactive Control

Investigating how informative cues about upcoming trials shape preparatory control of emotional conflict.

  • Paradigm: Symbolic cues predict whether the upcoming trial will be congruent or incongruent. Two cohorts perform the emotional conflict task while expecting conflict or non‑conflict trials.
  • Measurements: Behavioural accuracy and reaction times are complemented by eye‑tracking metrics (pupil dilation and saccadic latency) using a Gazepoint GP3 system to capture preparatory adjustments.
  • Analysis: Behavioural and oculometric data are compared across valid vs. invalid expectancy cues to quantify proactive control. Pupillometry and gaze patterns reveal anticipatory adjustments.
  • Findings: Informative cues reduce conflict costs and elicit early gaze shifts and pupil dilation, indicating that predictive information enables strategic up‑regulation of attention.

Uncertainty & Cognitive Control

Probing how unpredictability in reward structure influences control allocation and decision dynamics.

  • Paradigm: Modified conflict tasks systematically vary reward probability and outcome variance, producing blocks with high vs. low uncertainty about whether a correct response will be rewarded.
  • Measurements: Reaction times and accuracy across participants are fitted with hierarchical drift diffusion models (HDDM) to estimate latent parameters such as drift rate and boundary separation.
  • Analysis: Model‑based comparisons of high‑ and low‑uncertainty blocks reveal how uncertainty modulates evidence accumulation (drift) and caution (threshold). Behavioural conflict costs are assessed across conditions.
  • Findings: High uncertainty triggers more conservative decision thresholds and slower evidence accumulation, implying that unpredictability of rewards acts as a motivational signal for cognitive control.

Other Academic Projects

  • Suprathreshold stochastic resonance in two-compartment neurons – Simulated synaptic-noise gain in NEURON, revealing how noise can enhance signal detection in neuronal models.
  • Attention-linked modulation of SSVEP power – Re-analysis of open EEG datasets demonstrating ≥15% gain in 12 Hz occipital steady-state visual evoked potentials with focused attention.
  • Intrasexual competition in Psammophilus dorsalis – Behavioural analysis of 200 hours of video to construct state-transition matrices capturing lizard courtship dynamics.
