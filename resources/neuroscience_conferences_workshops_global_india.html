How to use this guide

Use conferences for different jobs

Big flagship meetings are best for exposure, networking, job hunting and broad field awareness. Specialist conferences are better for serious scientific feedback. Workshops and schools are often the highest return for students because they build skills and close mentoring gaps.

Do not chase “international” labels blindly

A smaller, society-backed workshop can be far more valuable than a generic international conference. Prioritise official societies, named programme committees, transparent abstract review, real speakers, travel awards, and a clear scientific community.

Searchable catalogue

Search by field, method, country, society, or tag. Examples: fMRI, emotion, India, computational, clinical, workshop, BCI.

India-specific strategy

Best default choices in India

For broad neuroscience, track Indian Academy of Neurosciences (IAN). For cognitive science/cognitive neuroscience, track ACCS. For clinical neurology, track IANCON. For neurosurgery/clinical neurological sciences, track NSICON. For molecular/neurochemistry, track SNCI. For workshops, track IISc, NIMHANS, NCBS, NBRC, IndiaBioscience and IBRO schools.

For students trying to enter neuroscience

Do not wait for a big international poster. First attend India-based workshops and society meetings, build a small project, learn methods, and meet labs. A good path is: ACCS/IAN → workshop/school → poster → global meeting.

Recommended pathways by profile

Undergrad / MSc entering neuroscience

Start with ACCS, IAN, IndiaBioscience-listed workshops, Neuromatch Academy, IBRO schools, IISc/NCBS/NIMHANS events. Then aim for SfN/FENS/IBRO if you have a poster.

Cognitive neuroscience PhD

India: ACCS + Bangalore Cognition Workshop + IAN. Global: CNS, OHBM, SANS/SAS, SPR, VSS, CCN, SfN. Pick based on whether your strongest contribution is behaviour, fMRI/EEG, emotion or modelling.

Computational neuroscience / neuroAI

Start with Neuromatch Academy, CNS*, CCN, COSYNE, Bernstein, INCF, Brainhack, and selected NeurIPS/ICLR/ICML workshops. In India, track ACCS, IISc brain-computation events, NCBS/IndiaBioscience listings.

Molecular/cellular/systems neuroscience

India: IAN, SNCI, NCBS/inStem workshops, IBRO schools. Global: SfN, FENS, CSHL meetings/courses, GRC/GRS, JNS NEURO, FAONS.

Clinical neuroscience / neurology

India: IANCON, NSICON, ECON, SLEEPCON/India Sleep, I-CNCNC, NIMHANS workshops. Global: AAN, EAN, AAIC, MDS Congress, ILAE congresses, SOBP/ECNP depending on disorder.

Neurotechnology / BCI / neuroengineering

Global: BRAIN Initiative Conference, IEEE EMBC, IEEE NER, Graz BCI, NeuroTechX listings, INCF, Brainhack. India: watch IIT/NIMHANS/defence/medical-device BCI workshops and IndiaBioscience listings.

Red flags: conferences to avoid

Many fake or low-value events use neuroscience, psychology, psychiatry, AI and neurology keywords to attract students. Before paying, check these red flags:

  • Conference name is extremely generic: ‘World Brain Summit’, ‘International Conference on Neuroscience and Neurology’ with no known society behind it.
  • Website promises Scopus/WoS publication as the main selling point rather than scientific programme quality.
  • Same organiser runs hundreds of unrelated conferences on the same dates/cities.
  • No real programme committee, no named speakers, or copied speaker photos.
  • Peer review takes 24–72 hours and acceptance seems automatic after payment.
  • Venue is vague, registration fee is unusually high, and refund/contact details are weak.
