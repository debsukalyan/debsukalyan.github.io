fNIRS resource

What is fNIRS?

Functional near-infrared spectroscopy is a non-invasive optical neuroimaging method. It uses near-infrared light to estimate task-related changes in oxygenated and deoxygenated haemoglobin near the cortical surface.

Its main strength is ecological flexibility: compared with fMRI, fNIRS can be more portable and easier to use during movement, social interaction, developmental studies, learning tasks, real-world cognition, and some rehabilitation contexts. Its main limitation is that it is strongest for superficial cortical signals and is sensitive to scalp/systemic physiology, motion, hair, optode coupling, and design choices.

Use fNIRS when your question needs:

  • Portable brain monitoringWorkload, attention, motor tasks, naturalistic behaviour, classroom or field-like settings.
  • Child / developmental compatibilityLess intimidating than MRI for many participants and often more tolerant of natural behaviour.
  • Social or interactive tasksHyperscanning, face-to-face communication, teaching-learning, cooperation and competition.
  • Complementary multimodal workCan be combined with behaviour, eye tracking, physiology, EEG, motion tracking, or clinical measures.

Beginner workflow

A practical route from idea to analysis. Treat this as a planning skeleton, not a substitute for a lab-specific protocol.

1QuestionDefine the cortical region, task contrast, timing, and expected haemodynamic response.
2DesignChoose block or event design, event markers, rest periods, control condition, and behavioural measures.
3MontagePlan source-detector layout, wavelengths, source-detector distance, optode labels, and short-separation channels if available.
4QualityCheck coupling, hair interference, motion, saturation, scalp signal, and subject comfort before recording.
5PreprocessConvert intensity to optical density, correct motion, filter carefully, convert to HbO/HbR, and model nuisance signals.
6ModelUse GLM/epochs/statistics, report HbO and HbR clearly, and correct for channels/ROIs where needed.

Study design checklist

Good fNIRS studies are often won or lost before analysis. These are the things beginners should force themselves to write down before collecting data.

Define primary contrast and ROI before data collection.
Record cap layout, optode positions, wavelengths, and source-detector distances.
Add behavioural measures so the brain signal has psychological meaning.
Plan short-separation / systemic physiology handling where possible.
Log exact task timings and event markers.
Document motion, hair/coupling issues, and exclusion rules.
Report preprocessing parameters, filtering choices, and motion correction method.
Keep raw data, scripts, and metadata organized using a reproducible folder structure.

A simple 2-week learning plan

For students who want a fast but serious entry into fNIRS.

Days 1–3

Understand the signal

Learn HbO/HbR, haemodynamic response, source-detector geometry, superficial physiology, motion artifacts, and why fNIRS is mostly cortical-surface sensitive.

Days 4–8

Run a tutorial

Follow an MNE or Homer3 tutorial end-to-end. Do not just read it; actually run a dataset, inspect channels, and plot HbO/HbR.

Days 9–14

Design a mini-study

Draft a one-page protocol: question, task, ROI, montage, contrasts, sample size logic, preprocessing, exclusion criteria, and planned statistics.
