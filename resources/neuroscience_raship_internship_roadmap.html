Neuroscience RAship / Internship Roadmap

How to find research assistantships & internships in neuroscience

A practical guide for students who want to enter neuroscience research: how to pick a research interest, find the right professors, build small proof projects, cold email properly, and search for positions across Indian and international portals.

1. Pick your research lane 2. Find the right professors 3. Build proof projects 4. Cold email well 5. Where to find openings 6. 30-day action plan

First: what exactly are you looking for?

Internship

Usually short-term exposure

Best if you are still exploring the field. You may help with literature review, basic coding, data cleaning, behavior experiments, participant recruitment, wet-lab work, or small analysis tasks.

  • Typical duration: 1–3 months, sometimes longer
  • Good for: UG / MSc / early-stage learners
  • Main aim: exposure + skills + clarity
RAship / project role

Usually deeper research work

A research assistantship is more structured. You may work on a funded project, collect data, run analyses, manage datasets, help with manuscripts, or contribute to experiments.

  • Typical duration: 6 months to 2 years
  • Good for: people preparing for PhD / MS / research career
  • Main aim: serious project experience + recommendation letters
Important: do not email everyone with “I am interested in neuroscience.” Make your interest specific enough that a professor can see where you might fit.

Step 1: start with your research interest

You do not need to know your exact thesis topic. But you should know the broad lane you are curious about. This makes the whole search much easier.

Cognitive / behavioural neuroscience

Mind, behaviour, emotion, decision-making

Focuses on how humans think, feel, decide, attend, remember, and behave.

Useful skills: psychology basics, experiments, statistics, R/Python, reaction-time analysis, questionnaires.

Example topics: attention, reward, emotion, mental health, decision-making, memory, perception.

Computational neuroscience

Models, coding, neural data

Uses maths, statistics, machine learning, and simulations to understand brain systems.

Useful skills: Python, linear algebra, probability, ML, dynamical systems, data visualization.

Example topics: neural networks, reinforcement learning, sensory coding, decision models, brain simulations.

Systems neuroscience

Circuits, behavior, physiology

Studies how neural circuits produce behaviour, often with animals or high-resolution recordings.

Useful skills: neurophysiology, behavior analysis, calcium imaging, electrophysiology, signal processing.

Example topics: navigation, motor control, sensory circuits, learning, sleep, decision circuits.

Neuroimaging

fMRI, EEG, MEG, brain networks

Studies the human brain using imaging and electrophysiology tools.

Useful skills: Python/MATLAB, signal processing, statistics, BIDS, Nilearn, MNE, AFNI/SPM/FSL.

Example topics: emotion, attention, language, resting-state networks, clinical imaging.

Molecular / cellular neuroscience

Cells, genes, proteins, disease mechanisms

Studies neurons and glia at molecular and cellular levels. This is a natural entry route for biology, biotechnology, microbiology, biochemistry, and life-science students.

Useful skills: molecular biology, microscopy, cell culture, immunostaining, transcriptomics, animal models.

Example topics: synapses, neurodegeneration, regeneration, neuroinflammation, gene expression, disease models.

Neurotech / BCI / clinical

Brain tools + translational work

Bridges neuroscience with engineering, medicine, rehabilitation, and digital health.

Useful skills: sensors, EEG, ML, UI/tools, clinical data, documentation, ethics.

Example topics: BCI, sleep, epilepsy, rehabilitation, computational psychiatry, digital biomarkers.

How to make your interest more specific

Start with this sentence and fill the blanks:

I am interested in [broad area] because I want to understand [specific question/problem]. I am especially curious about labs that use [method/tool/model system] to study [population/process/disorder].

Example: “I am interested in cognitive neuroscience because I want to understand how reward changes attention and emotion regulation. I am especially curious about labs that use behavioural experiments, fMRI, EEG, and computational models.”

Step 2: find the right professors and labs

Look for fit, not just famous names. A smaller lab that closely matches your interest is often better than a big-name lab where your email is generic.

Where to start

Build a lab shortlist

  1. Start from department or centre websites.
  2. Open each professor’s lab page.
  3. Read the “Research” section carefully.
  4. Open 2–3 recent papers from the lab.
  5. Note the methods, model systems, datasets, and open positions.
  6. Shortlist 10 labs that actually fit.

Step 3: build proof that you can contribute

A professor is more likely to reply when your email shows evidence: a small project, a notebook, a poster, a literature summary, or a clean analysis. It does not need to be perfect. It needs to be relevant.

Cognitive / behaviour project

Run a small behavioural experiment

Build a Stroop task, emotional Stroop task, memory task, attention task, or decision-making task using PsychoPy, jsPsych, or Gorilla-style logic.

  • Collect pilot data from friends or demo participants.
  • Plot reaction time and accuracy.
  • Write a 1-page summary: question, task, result, limitation.
PsychoPy · jsPsych
EEG / MEG project

Analyze an open EEG dataset

Use MNE-Python and an open dataset to learn event markers, epochs, ERPs, time-frequency plots, and basic preprocessing.

  • Pick one dataset from OpenNeuro.
  • Make an ERP plot for two conditions.
  • Explain what preprocessing steps you used.
MNE-Python · OpenNeuro
fMRI / imaging project

Make a beginner neuroimaging notebook

Use Nilearn to load a sample brain image, visualize brain maps, understand masks, and compare task contrasts if the dataset provides them.

  • Start with Nilearn examples.
  • Make clean visuals of brain regions or activation maps.
  • Write what fMRI can and cannot tell us.
Nilearn · OpenNeuro
Computational neuroscience project

Build a model or reproduce a small result

Simulate a neuron, train a simple neural network, model a decision process, or reproduce a figure from a computational neuroscience tutorial.

  • Use Python notebooks.
  • Explain the model assumptions clearly.
  • Do not oversell it: show what the model captures and misses.
Neuromatch Computational Neuroscience · Neuronal Dynamics
Molecular neuroscience project

Do a literature + database mini-project

Pick a disease or process such as Alzheimer’s disease, synaptic plasticity, neuroinflammation, axon regeneration, or dopaminergic neurons. Use public papers and databases to build a small map of genes, proteins, pathways, or cell types.

  • Use PubMed to collect 5–8 key papers.
  • Use Allen Brain Atlas for expression / region context.
  • Use STRING or GeneCards to explore gene networks.
  • Make one pathway diagram or gene-expression summary.
PubMed · Allen Brain Atlas · STRING · GeneCards
Neurotech / BCI project

Build a simple signal-processing demo

If you have engineering or coding experience, show signal-processing thinking: filtering, feature extraction, classification, or a small dashboard for biosignals.

  • Use open EEG/BCI datasets.
  • Show raw signal → filtered signal → feature → simple classifier.
  • Keep the explanation non-hype and grounded.
BNCI Horizon datasets · MNE
How to show it: make a GitHub repository, Google Colab notebook, one-page PDF, or short carousel explaining the question, data/resource, analysis, figure, interpretation, and limitation.

Step 4: cold email without sounding generic

The goal of a cold email is not to tell your life story. It is to quickly show fit, evidence, and seriousness.

What to include

A good cold email has 5 parts

  1. Who you are and your current stage.
  2. Why their lab specifically interests you.
  3. One sentence showing you read their work.
  4. Your relevant skills or small proof project.
  5. A clear ask: internship, RAship, project role, or meeting.
What to avoid

Common mistakes

  • “I am passionate about neuroscience” with no specific fit.
  • Attaching a 6-page generic CV with no context.
  • Emailing 50 professors with the same text.
  • Asking for a PhD position when you want a short internship.
  • Not mentioning what you can actually contribute.

Cold email template: internship / project exposure

Subject: Inquiry about internship/project opportunity in [lab topic] Dear Prof. [Name], I am [your name], currently [your program/year] at [institution]. I am interested in [specific research area], especially [specific topic/question]. I read about your lab’s work on [specific project/paper/topic]. I found [one specific thing] particularly interesting because [short reason]. I have been building relevant skills in [Python/statistics/behavioural experiments/EEG/fMRI/molecular biology/etc.]. As a small proof of interest, I worked on [brief project: 1 line + link if available]. I wanted to ask if there may be any possibility of contributing to your lab as a short-term intern / project student. I would be happy to help with [specific tasks you can realistically do: literature review, data cleaning, analysis, experiment coding, wet-lab assistance, etc.]. I have attached my CV and included a short project link below. Thank you for your time. Best regards, [Your name] [Program, institution] [Email] [GitHub/portfolio/LinkedIn link]

Cold email template: RAship

Subject: Inquiry about RA/project assistant opportunities in [research area] Dear Prof. [Name], I am [your name], [current degree / recently graduated from]. I am looking for a research assistant/project role in [specific field], and your lab’s work on [specific topic] aligns closely with my interests. I was particularly interested in your recent work on [paper/project]. My understanding is that your lab studies [one-sentence summary]. I am interested in contributing because [fit with your background]. My relevant experience includes: • [Skill/experience 1] • [Skill/experience 2] • [Small project or paper summary] • [Tools: Python/R/MATLAB/PsychoPy/MNE/Nilearn/molecular techniques/etc.] If there are any current or upcoming RA/project assistant positions, I would be grateful to be considered. I have attached my CV and included links to relevant work. Thank you for your time. Best regards, [Your name]

Follow-up email

Subject: Follow-up: internship/RA inquiry in [topic] Dear Prof. [Name], I hope you are doing well. I wanted to gently follow up on my earlier email regarding a possible internship/RA opportunity in your lab. I understand you may be busy, so I just wanted to check whether there may be any possibility to contribute to ongoing or upcoming work in [specific area]. Thank you again for your time. Best regards, [Your name]

Step 5: prepare a sharp one-page CV

For early-stage students, a clean one-page CV is usually better than a long unfocused CV.

What to highlight

CV sections that matter

  • Education and current stage
  • Research interests in 2–3 lines
  • Relevant coursework
  • Technical skills
  • Research/project experience
  • Public links: GitHub, portfolio, poster, notebook
  • References only if requested
Skill examples

Write skills specifically

Bad“Python, neuroscience, hard-working.”
Better“Python: pandas, matplotlib, basic statistics; MNE beginner EEG analysis; PsychoPy task design.”
Bad“Interested in brain research.”
Better“Interested in reward, attention, emotion, and cognitive control using behaviour + fMRI/EEG.”

Use a mix of official institute pages, opportunity aggregators, LinkedIn/Twitter, and direct lab websites. Some positions are formally advertised; many are found by emailing the right professor at the right time.

30-day action plan

Week 1

Pick lane + build tracker

  • Choose 2–3 research themes.
  • Write 5 keywords for each theme.
  • Make a spreadsheet of labs and PIs.
  • Save 3 papers from each shortlisted lab.
Week 2

Build one proof project

  • Pick one project idea related to your lane.
  • Create a small analysis / literature map / notebook.
  • Make 1–2 clean figures or summaries.
  • Write a short README.
Week 3

Prepare materials

  • Make a one-page CV.
  • Write 2 email templates.
  • Customize each email for the lab.
  • Upload project proof to GitHub/Drive/portfolio.
Week 4

Apply and follow up

  • Email 8–12 carefully selected labs.
  • Apply to 3–5 listed opportunities.
  • Track replies and follow up after 7–10 days.
  • Keep improving your project while waiting.

Final checklist before emailing

  • Did I mention the professor’s specific research area?
  • Did I show that I read at least one paper/project from the lab?
  • Did I clearly say whether I want an internship, RAship, or project role?
  • Did I include my CV?
  • Did I link one relevant project or proof of skill?
  • Is the email under 250–300 words?
  • Is the subject line specific?

Reminder: This guide is meant to help you approach neuroscience opportunities in a structured way. Always check the official institute or lab page for eligibility, deadlines, and current availability.
