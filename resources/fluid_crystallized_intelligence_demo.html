Fluid vs Crystallized Intelligence

A short interactive demo showing the difference between solving new patterns and using learned verbal/conceptual knowledge.

Important: This is not Raven’s Progressive Matrices, not WAIS, and not a diagnostic IQ test. It uses original demo items inspired by common task formats: matrix-pattern reasoning for fluid intelligence and vocabulary/general-knowledge items for crystallized intelligence.

What are we testing?

~5–8 minutes

Fluid intelligence is your ability to solve new problems, spot patterns, and reason when the answer is not already memorized.

Crystallized intelligence is your ability to use knowledge, vocabulary, concepts, and information accumulated through learning and experience.

This demo is designed for educational/social-media use — for example, to show why “intelligence” is not one single school-subject hierarchy.

Method Note

For educational use

This demo is based on the distinction between fluid and crystallized intelligence. The fluid section uses original matrix-style items because matrix reasoning tasks are commonly used to assess abstract, nonverbal reasoning. The crystallized section uses original vocabulary, similarity, and general-knowledge-style items because crystallized intelligence depends more on accumulated language, knowledge, and conceptual learning.

It intentionally avoids real Raven’s Progressive Matrices and WAIS items. Real standardized tests require licensed materials, controlled administration, validated norms, and professional interpretation.

References and background

  • Cattell, R. B. (1963). Theory of fluid and crystallized intelligence: A critical experiment. Journal of Educational Psychology, 54(1), 1–22.
  • Horn, J. L., & Cattell, R. B. (1966/1967). Work extending the theory of fluid and crystallized intelligence across development and aging.
  • Raven’s Progressive Matrices are widely described as nonverbal pattern-reasoning tasks used to estimate abstract/fluid reasoning.
  • WAIS-style verbal comprehension tasks often include vocabulary, similarities, and general information, reflecting learned verbal/conceptual knowledge.
  • Use this only as a classroom, outreach, or social-media demonstration — not for diagnosis, selection, or claims about a person’s “real IQ.”
