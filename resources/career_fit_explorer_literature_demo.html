Career exploration demo · 8–12 minutes

Find career paths that fit how you like to think, work, and grow.

This is a lightweight, literature-grounded demo. It asks people to respond to simple activity cards, work-value trade-offs, confidence questions, and workplace-style prompts. It then ranks broad career families and explains why each path may fit.

Why these questions?

The demo combines four ideas that are common in career psychology. Each is simplified so that the experience feels like a quick activity rather than a long psychometric test.

01

Interests: “What work naturally attracts you?”

The activity cards are organized around the RIASEC model: Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, and Conventional interests. These are widely used in career exploration tools such as the O*NET Interest Profiler.

02

Work values: “What would make work feel worth it?”

The work-value prompts are inspired by the O*NET Work Importance Locator and Theory of Work Adjustment: Achievement, Independence, Recognition, Relationships, Support, and Working Conditions.

03

Self-efficacy: “Where do you feel capable enough to try?”

Social Cognitive Career Theory argues that career interests and choices are shaped by self-efficacy, expected outcomes, goals, learning experiences, and contextual support. This demo therefore asks about confidence, but does not treat confidence as a replacement for skill.

04

Work style: “What environment helps you stay engaged?”

The final prompts use broad, non-diagnostic style signals such as novelty, depth, structure, collaboration, visibility, and pressure tolerance. These are included as practical fit signals, not as a formal personality inventory.

Scientific caveat: Career fit is probabilistic, not destiny. A high match means “this path may be worth exploring,” not “you should do this forever.” Access, training, mentorship, money, health, family constraints, discrimination, local job markets, and chance all shape real career outcomes.

How to use this safely

Use the output to start a conversation, shortlist internships, choose people to interview, or plan one-week “try-before-you-commit” experiments. Do not use it to label people permanently.

  • Say “high fit right now,” not “your true destiny.”
  • Show multiple possible careers, not one fixed answer.
  • Include growth edges so people see how to build fit.
  • Encourage real-world trials: shadowing, mini-projects, coursework, volunteering, internships.

How to improve this later

For a more rigorous version, collect validation data: Do the recommendations predict which paths people later explore, enjoy, persist in, or perform well in? You can also add local labour-market data, salary ranges, training routes, and examples of Indian/UK/global pathways.

A better version would also separate interest, skill, access, and values instead of collapsing everything into one score.

Research base and sources used

  1. Holland, J. L. RIASEC theory of vocational personalities and work environments. Used here as the six-interest structure: Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional.
  2. O*NET Interest Profiler, National Center for O*NET Development. It measures the six RIASEC occupational interests and links interest profiles to occupational exploration. Source
  3. O*NET Work Importance Locator User’s Guide. Work values are framed as Achievement, Independence, Recognition, Relationships, Support, and Working Conditions, grounded in Theory of Work Adjustment. Source
  4. Lent, R. W., Brown, S. D., & Hackett, G. Social Cognitive Career Theory. Used here for the idea that self-efficacy, outcome expectations, goals, and contexts shape interests and choices.
  5. Nye, C. D., Su, R., Rounds, J., & Drasgow, F. (2012). Vocational interests and performance: A quantitative summary of over 60 years of research. Perspectives on Psychological Science.
  6. Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. Self-Determination Theory. Used here only as background for why autonomy, competence, and relatedness can matter for motivation and engagement.
  7. Big Five personality literature. Used here only as a cautionary influence: broad traits can matter, but this demo does not administer or interpret a validated Big Five inventory.
